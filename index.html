The Stories We Unconsciously Tell

Gone Home is a game that perfects the art of telling you a story without telling you the story. It's a game that requires no twitch reflexes, honed FPS skills, or rapid hand movement. Instead it relies on the oldest tradition of all: narrative. With its excellent story telling and self-exploration motif, Gone Home proves that sometimes the greatest discoveries are made at home.

You play as Katie Greenbriar, a college student who's just returned home from a trip abroad in Europe. While you were away your family moved into a nice mansion and it's your first time visiting. However, when you get to the front door you find that the door is locked and nobody is home. On the door is a note from your younger sister, Samantha, which simply says "I'm sorry I can't be there to see you, but it's impossible. Please, please don't go digging around trying to find out where I am." This just begs you to do so.


Gone Home is a game about exploring the leftovers of daily life. Discarded notes, journal entries, old letters all form the basis of the story. As you discover these mementos of what's happened since you've been gone, Samantha narrates the story behind each one giving you a glimpse of her high school life. Other plot threads are unraveled by sifting through photos, postcards, and mail gathering dust around the house.

The gameplay is unique in its minimalism. There's no shooting, running, or slashing in Gone Home; you simply pick up objects, rotate them around, examine their details, and play the occasional mixtape. The little interaction you have with objects in the game forces you instead to pay attention to your surroundings, all the hints are in reading and listening to the narrative. By keeping your choices limited, Gone Home makes it so you can't get distracted by unrelated choices. Everything you pick up tells part of the story. I found myself compulsively opening every drawer and scouring every room in order to ensure that I didn't miss a single detail.

As you pick up items around the house, you begin to learn small things about your family. Discovering your father's old manuscripts and deciphering his old novelist career, or discovering a cheat sheet for Street Fighter II that Sam used to get better to face her friends. As you pick up these objects, Katie inserts her own commentary at points, such as an appalled reaction to finding one of her father's Playboy magazine. These small touches add to the feeling that you're truly a part of a family and enhance the small scope of the game. It's much like the audiotapes found in Bioshock or the books you pick up in Skyrim, except in Gone Home these form the entire story, instead of fleshing out the world. By doing so the game raises environmental storytelling from simply a sort of side-hobby for players heavily invested in lore and instead makes it the key element to show how games can do so much with narrative without outright telling you the story. It's up to you to discover clues and hints so you can draw your own conclusion about what happened while you were away instead of someone showing up and conveniently telling you everything in 10 minutes.



Gone Home's charm is its success in recreating 90's nostalgia. It's not the typical "shove the time-period" down your throat approach featuring newspapers with obviously period-specific headlines or TV programs on public perception of the war. Instead the game places you in time by using real artifacts of that time, such as VHS tapes, Super Nintendo games lying about, and a healthy dose of punk rock. Although some younger players might go though the game picking up a cassette tape and wondering how to even play, for those who lived in the '90's I imagine the game brings back memories of listening to mixtapes made by friends and renting videos from your local Blockbuster. (Having not quite been a "'90's" kid I can't really comment.)

The sense of home is also an important aspect of Gone Home. It's something familiar, not a foreign planet raided by aliens or a western tavern in the 1800's. The developers of the game paid excruciating detail in order to nail the "homey" feel everyone experiences when walking through their front door. Blanket forts, closets filled with unpacked boxes, old homework assignments from grade school, discarded pizza boxes, notes left on the fridge. All of these small points really make it feel like somebody lives here and allows you to connect with the game's environment better.



It's also incredibly easy to sympathize with Samantha, whose story is revealed to use bit by bit by exploring the house. By peeking through her notes, you see how she starts off as a nervous freshman student wanting to be all but invisible, but slowly evolves into a rebellious teen coming into her own. Despite never meeting her, Samantha becomes a fully fleshed out character, a teenager having a difficult time transitioning to high school life, with all the anxieties, societal pressure, and hormones that comes with it. Samantha becomes a character you can empathize with. Usually it is all too easy to write off teenagers as "crazy" or "not like our day", but Gone Home excellently crafts Samantha as a character who can remind you of your teenage years. The disinterest in academics, the thrill of sneaking out at night, a first kiss, it's all documented in Samantha's journal and her stories can evoke your own from your high school past. It is too easy to dismiss your old self as foolish or stupid because for some reason the feelings or thoughts you had when growing up were somehow less valid or not as informed as they are now, but Gone Home reminds you that the feelings you felt back then were just as raw and real as they are now, and that you should embrace how you felt as part of growing up.

Gone Home was initially advertised as a horror game, perhaps in order to attract gamers who wouldn't have been interested in such a story-heavy game. The stormy night and the pitch-black rooms reminded me of when I was kid, walking around my house at night scared of what might be lurking behind the couch or under the table. That is, until I hit the light and was welcomed with the familiar sight of my family room or kitchen and immediately felt cozy, wondering why I was scared of being in my own home in the first place. Gone Home nails this feeling, with every room you visit you can't help feel a bit scared that something or someone might be lurking in the dark, but after you turn on the switch you see a snug TV room or a study filled with musty books. The "horror" in the game is not horror, but rather suspense, you sometimes feel as if the developers are trying to pull one over you and that some sort of grotesque monster may come out of the dark at any time. This distrust of the developers actually enhances the game I, as you end up with a game where the "horror" is all in your head, it's simply a result of the atmosphere that was expertly crafted into the game.


It's difficult to criticize a game that excelled in creating an empty home atmosphere, but at the beginning of the game I felt it was somewhat contrived how the game blocks you off of accessing certain wings of the house with a key. It's the oldest game cliche in the book, and I thought the developers would have done better to have just allowed you to explore the entire house at once instead of coming up with this simple puzzle for such an emotionally complicated game. The puzzles are nothing difficult, and at one point in the game I found myself stuck wandering around to find a clue to advance in the game. It wasn't inherently obvious where I was supposed to find the clue, nor was finding it all that rewarding as I found it via a lucky click at the right spot. At some points I felt the game got caught up a bit in its own narrative which made it harder for me to keep track of where the next clue might be, but overall I was completely engrossed in the story Gone Home was telling that I didn't care too much.

Overall


Gone Home is a game that brings narrative to the forefront of the gaming experience. It's well-written, witty, heart-tearing story that is one of the best of any game I've played, and it does it all without another soul in the house. Although some may debate about it's status as a game, Gone Home's' immersive atmosphere and sense of exploration is something that could have only been accomplished in this medium. By telling a story through the environment, it enables you to imagine your interpretations of what exactly happened to your family while giving you structure and it also gives you time to reflect upon what you learn. It's a game that silently pulls you through its paces, and it never feels forced or contrived in the way it does so. Gone Home proves that games don't need thrilling cutscenes or expansive scifi worlds to be an exciting game, rather that an excellent story and a well-crafted environment can make you feel more satisfied with a game taking place at home than any game about space aliens or war zones.
